# Monthly_Order_Count_Time_Series_Analysis
Supply Chain Demand Forecasting

A comparative time series study that forecasts monthly delivery demand for a
supply chain operation. The project benchmarks a full range of methods — from
simple seasonal regressions to exponential smoothing and Seasonal ARIMA — and
selects the model that best supports short-term demand planning.

------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. OVERVIEW
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

In Supply Chain Management (SCM), demand forecasting is the lever that
simultaneously minimizes inventory cost and maximizes service level. Delivery
volume is a direct proxy for operational activity, so an accurate forecast
becomes the basis for resource allocation and operational scheduling.

The goal is to build a forecasting model optimized for a complex, seasonal time
series and to recommend the single best model for predicting demand at least 10
periods ahead. Models are compared on a held-out test set using RMSE and MAPE.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2. DATA
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Source file : supply_chain_deliveries.csv
Coverage    : 2020-01-02 ~ 2025-06-30  (~5.5 years)

Raw variables:
  - WorkDate     : delivery date
  - Customer     : key accounts (Amazon, Costco, Home Depot, ...)
  - Location     : hub location (Chicago, Los Angeles, ...)
  - OrderCount   : number of orders in the delivery
  - TotalRevenue : total revenue generated by the delivery

Target variable:
  - Monthly Total OrderCount

Daily data is noisy and highly volatile, so it is resampled to a MONTHLY level,
which is more appropriate for strategic decision-making and stabilizes the
analysis.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. EDA & PREPROCESSING
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Findings from EDA:
  - The series shows a clear upward TREND: the mean rises over time.
  - Variance grows with the level (heteroscedasticity). Rolling-std and a
    mean-vs-std scatter plot both confirm a positive mean/variance relationship.
  - Seasonal decomposition reveals a strong, recurring 12-month seasonal cycle
    on top of the increasing trend.

Preprocessing steps:
  - Aggregation       : daily OrderCount -> monthly OrderCount (reduces noise,
                        enables stable analysis).
  - Missing values    : linear interpolation (fill with the mean of adjacent
                        months) to preserve the continuity of the series.
  - Log transformation: applied to stabilize the growing variance. After the log
                        transform the fluctuation band becomes roughly constant.
  - Feature engineering for the regression-based models (dummies / harmonics).


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
4. METHODOLOGY
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Six modeling families are implemented and evaluated. The last 12 months are
held out as the test set; metrics are RMSE and MAPE.

(1) Binary Variable Method
    Monthly dummy variables capture seasonality. December is used as the base
    group to avoid multicollinearity, leaving 11 dummy variables. A linear
    trend term models the upward movement.

(2) Trigonometric (Fourier) Method
    Seasonality is modeled as continuous sin/cos terms. Two variants are
    compared: 1 harmonic vs. 2 harmonics. The richer 2-harmonic model captures
    more of the seasonal shape.

(3) Exponential Smoothing — SES & DES
    Simple Exponential Smoothing (level only) and Double Exponential Smoothing
    (level + trend). A grid search sweeps alpha (and beta for DES) from 0.1 to
    0.9. Neither method models seasonality, so they serve as a baseline.

(4) Holt-Winters — Additive & Multiplicative
    Triple exponential smoothing integrating level, trend, and seasonality.
      - Additive       : assumes constant seasonal amplitude.
      - Multiplicative : assumes seasonal amplitude scales with the level.
    Because the data shows variance growing with level, the multiplicative form
    is expected (and confirmed) to fit better.

(5) Seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA)
    Box-Jenkins methodology: stationarity testing, differencing, ACF/PACF
    inspection, and grid search over (p,d,q)(P,D,Q,12). Two selection criteria
    are contrasted — minimum AIC vs. minimum test MAPE.

(6) ARIMA via the full Box-Jenkins procedure (taught classroom workflow)
    Step 1 Preprocessing : confirm non-stationarity via ACF (slow decay), apply
                           first differencing (d=1) and seasonal differencing
                           (D=1, s=12) until the series is stationary.
    Step 2/3 Estimation  : identify candidate orders from ACF/PACF, then choose
                           the model with the lowest AIC.
    Step 4 Diagnostics   : residual ACF inspected against a 3-sigma band; if most
                           bars fall inside, the model is judged Adequate.
    Step 5 Forecast      : forecast 12 periods, back-transform to the original
                           scale, and report point forecasts with 95% intervals.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
5. KEY RESULTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Seasonal-regression baselines (12-month test set):

    Model                          RMSE        MAPE
    ----------------------------   --------    -------
    Binary Variable                2081.96     3.03%
    Trigonometric (1 harmonic)     5898.09     7.66%
    Trigonometric (2 harmonics)    5102.87     6.51%

The Binary Variable model strongly outperforms the trigonometric models. With
11 dummies it has more explanatory power than the 4 harmonic terms, and it can
follow the spiky, non-smooth peaks/troughs that the smooth Fourier curves miss.

Exponential smoothing (no seasonality — best grid-search MAPE):

    SES  best : alpha=0.1            MAPE ~ 25.05%
    DES  best : alpha=0.1, beta~0.2  MAPE ~ 24.98%

SES and DES perform poorly and barely differ from each other, confirming that
this series is dominated by seasonality rather than a simple linear trend.

Advanced seasonal models (12-month test set):

    Model                          RMSE        MAPE
    ----------------------------   --------    -------
    Additive Holt-Winters          4449.83     5.67%
    Multiplicative Holt-Winters    2194.51     3.22%
    SARIMA (1,1,1)(1,0,1,12)       2559.23     3.73%

  - Multiplicative HW is the best advanced model: it tracks the peaks and
    troughs closely because the data's seasonal amplitude genuinely scales with
    the level (multiplicative structure).
  - SARIMA is a close runner-up.
  - Additive HW under-estimates demand in the high-volume 2025 test window
    because it wrongly assumes constant seasonal amplitude.

SARIMA model-selection contrast:

    Criterion            Order                       AIC        Test MAPE
    ------------------   -------------------------   -------    ---------
    Minimum AIC          (0,0,0)(1,0,0,12)           -128.47    4.24%
    Minimum MAPE         (1,1,1)(1,0,1,12)           -109.75    3.25%

Box-Jenkins ARIMA (selected by minimum AIC among the differenced candidates):

    Final model : ARIMA (0,1,1)(0,1,1,12),  AIC = -114.13
    d=1  removes trend ; D=1,s=12 removes the 12-month seasonal cycle ;
    MA(1) and seasonal MA(1) capture the short-run error structure.
    Residual ACF stays inside the 3-sigma band -> model is adequate (white noise).


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. WHY MAPE OVER AIC
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

AIC favors statistically parsimonious, robust models for long-horizon stability.
MAPE directly targets out-of-sample forecast error. For supply-chain delivery
demand, where the operational need is accurate short-term (next ~12 months)
forecasting, the MAPE-optimal model is the more sensible business choice. The
manual MAPE-driven Holt-Winters search also beat statsmodels' optimized=True
fit, because optimized=True minimizes in-sample SSE (risking overfitting) while
the manual search optimizes out-of-sample generalization.


------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. CONCLUSIONS / INSIGHTS
------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. Trend & seasonality alignment — the seasonal models successfully learn the
     long-term trend and the 12-month rhythm, not just a flat average.
  2. Best model — Multiplicative Holt-Winters (MAPE 3.22%) is recommended as the
     primary forecaster; the Binary Variable regression (MAPE 3.03%) is an
     equally strong, interpretable alternative; SARIMA is a robust backup.
  3. Risk management — SARIMA 95% confidence intervals widen with horizon,
     signaling the need for more flexibility in longer-range planning.
  4. Operational use — the upper bound of the forecast interval is a direct
     basis for safety-stock sizing, minimizing stock-out risk instead of
     planning to the mean alone.
  5. Reliability — residual diagnostics confirm white noise, so the forecasts
     fully exploit the time-series information and are defensible as an
     objective input to management decisions.
